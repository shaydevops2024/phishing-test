How to spot a phishing attack

You just went through a simulated phishing test — no harm done. Here's how you could have caught it, using the exact signs from the email and page you just saw.

The red flags in this attack

1

The sender's name didn't match the email

The email came from uraih.m@iconductcloud.com. The real colleague is Uriah — but the address spelled it "uraih.m", with the letters scrambled. Attackers register look-alike or misspelled addresses hoping you won't read them closely. Always check the full email address, not just the display name.

2

The web address (URL) was wrong

A real company login never lives on a public free-hosting domain. This link used something like dotpr0ject.…duckdns.org / …nip.io — note the "0" instead of "o" in "dotpr0ject" and the duckdns / nip.io ending. The genuine DotProject SSO lives on the official internal company address. Read the URL in the address bar carefully before typing anything.

3

The login page didn't look like the real one

The fake page was a plain "UNITASK GROUP — verify your info" form. The real DotProject-SSO sign-in page looks like this:

☝ The genuine page: "DOTPROJECT-SSO", "Sign in to your account", with the usual SSO buttons. If the page you land on doesn't match the one you normally use, stop.

Your quick checklist before clicking or logging in

  • Hover over links to preview the real destination before clicking.
  • Read the full sender address — watch for misspellings and look-alikes.
  • Check the URL bar: is it the exact, official company domain (no duckdns/nip.io, no "0"-for-"o")?
  • Never enter your password on a page you reached from an email link — go to the site directly instead.
  • When in doubt, don't click. Verify with the sender through a known channel.
  • Report anything suspicious — even if you're not sure.

When you're not sure — ask

If anything about a message, link, or login page seems off and you can't be completely certain it's genuine, do not proceed. Before entering any credentials or clicking through, reach out to the company's DevOps or IT team and confirm that it's legitimate.

There is no such thing as a silly question here — the team would far rather answer a quick "is this real?" than deal with a compromised account. When in doubt, check first.
